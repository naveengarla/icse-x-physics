**Professor Magnus:** "The balance point shifts *toward* where more mass is. Now apply that thinking to the boomerang."

## 🧠 Deep Explanation — Feynman Style

### Step 1: Building the Idea from Atoms

Every object is made of tiny particles — atoms. Each atom has a tiny weight (mass × gravity) pulling it straight downward.

The total weight of the object is the sum of all these tiny weights. But when does the whole object tip, balance, or fall?

It tips whenever the *net turning effect* of all these tiny weights is non-zero about the support point.

**Centre of Gravity (CG):** The single imaginary point where, if you applied a single upward force equal to the total weight, the object would have zero net turning effect — it would balance perfectly.

In other words: the CG is the point where all the individual weight-moments cancel, leaving the object with no preference to rotate.

---

### Step 2: Why the CG Can Be in Empty Space

Think of the boomerang's two arms as two separate weights at two separate positions. The CG of the whole system lies between them — weighted toward the heavier/longer arm.

If both arms are equal in mass and equal in length from the joint, the CG is exactly between them — which happens to be in the air.

**Mira:** "So the object doesn't need to be solid everywhere for the CG to act as a valid balance point. The math just finds the 'average position of mass'?"

**Professor Magnus:** "Precisely. The CG is the **mass-weighted average position** of all the material. It's a mathematical average, not a physical location. It doesn't need to contain any material."

---

### Step 3: CG of Uniform Regular Objects

For objects with uniform density (material evenly distributed) and regular geometric shapes, the CG is always at the **geometric centre** — the centre of symmetry:

| Object | Position of CG |
|:---|:---|
| Uniform rod | Midpoint (centre of length) |
| Uniform rectangular plate | Intersection of diagonals |
| Uniform circular disc | Geometric centre |
| Uniform ring | Centre of the ring (in the hole) |
| Uniform sphere | Geometric centre |
| Uniform triangular plate | Point of intersection of medians (centroid) — at 1/3 from each base |
| Uniform square/rectangle | Intersection of diagonals |
| Uniform cylinder | Midpoint of the axis |
| Solid cone | On the axis, at height $h/4$ from the base |
| Hollow cone | On the axis, at height $h/3$ from the base |

---

### Step 4: CG and Balance — The Critical Relationship

**Arjun:** "So if I support an object exactly at its CG, it stays perfectly balanced?"

**Professor Magnus:** "Yes — if you apply a single upward force exactly at the CG equal to the object's weight, the object has no tendency to rotate. It is in perfect equilibrium."

**Mira:** "And if you move the support away from the CG, the object tilts — because now the weight moment is non-zero."

**Professor Magnus:** "Exactly. The CG is the only support point that produces zero net moment."

---

### Step 5: Stability — When Does an Object Fall Over?

Here's a critical real-world application: when does an object tip over?

**The Stability Rule:** An object is stable (won't topple) as long as the **vertical line drawn downward through its CG falls within its base of support**.

The moment that vertical line falls *outside* the base — the object falls.

```
Stable:              Unstable (about to tip):

   △ CG                  CG △
   |                         |
   |                          |
   |_________|           _____|
   base (wide)              base
   line through CG         line through CG falls
   is inside base          OUTSIDE base → TIPS
```

**Why racing cars are wide and low:**
- Wide base → harder for the CG line to exit the base
- Low CG → requires more tilt before the CG line exits

**Why books stack stably when flat but fall when stacked sideways on a narrow edge:**
- Flat position: base is large, CG line easily stays inside
- Narrow edge: base is tiny, a small CG shift exits the base → topples

### Step 6: Finding the CG of an Irregular Shape — The Plumb Line Method

How do you find the CG of a weirdly shaped cardboard piece — a leaf, a country outline, an odd bracket?

**The Plumb Line (Experimental) Method:**

**Apparatus:** Irregular lamina (flat shape), pin and cork, plumb line (string + small weight), pencil, stand.

**Procedure:**
1. Make 3 small holes at well-separated points near the edge of the lamina (label A, B, C).
2. Suspend the lamina freely from hole A using a pin through a cork attached to a stand.
3. The lamina hangs freely — it will orient itself so its CG is directly below the pin.
4. Hang a plumb line from the same pin. The plumb line points directly downward.
5. Mark two points on the plumb line's position on the lamina. Draw the plumb line (line $\ell_1$).
6. Repeat by suspending from hole B → draw line $\ell_2$.
7. Repeat from hole C → draw line $\ell_3$.
8. The three lines ($\ell_1$, $\ell_2$, $\ell_3$) all intersect at a single point — this is the **Centre of Gravity**.

**Why this works:** When suspended from any point, the CG hangs directly below the suspension point (because then the only moment is zero — the object is in equilibrium). The plumb line traces this vertical line. Any two such vertical lines cross at the CG.

## 🔍 Critical Thinking Corner

| Scenario | Effect on CG |
|:---|:---|
| A hollow cone vs a solid cone — same base, same height? | Hollow cone CG is higher ($h/3$) than solid cone ($h/4$) — material is in the walls, not the base |
| A brick lying flat vs standing on its narrow end? | CG position relative to ground changes — standing brick is more easily toppled |
| You add a heavy weight to one end of a uniform rod? | CG shifts toward the heavy end |
| A bus carries all passengers to the upper deck? | CG rises → more easily toppled during a sharp turn |
| A racing car driver is replaced by a heavier driver? | CG shifts upward and toward heavier side → affects stability |

> **Hidden insight:** Stability is not about how heavy an object is. It's about **where the CG is relative to the base**. A very heavy object with a high CG can be more unstable than a light object with a low, wide base.

## 📘 Formal Conclusion — ICSE Board Ready

### ✅ Centre of Gravity (Definition)

> The **centre of gravity (CG)** of a body is defined as the point through which the resultant of the gravitational forces on all particles of the body acts, irrespective of the orientation of the body.

*Or equivalently:*

> The CG is the single point where the **entire weight of the body is assumed to act**.

---

### ✅ CG of Regular Uniform Objects (ICSE Table)

| Object | Position of CG |
|:---|:---|
| Uniform straight rod | Midpoint of the rod |
| Uniform rectangular/square lamina | Intersection of its diagonals |
| Uniform circular disc | Geometric centre |
| Uniform ring | Centre of the ring |
| Uniform triangular lamina | Intersection of the medians (centroid) |
| Uniform solid sphere | Geometric centre |
| Uniform solid cylinder | Midpoint of its axis |
| Uniform solid cone | On axis, $\frac{h}{4}$ from base |
| Uniform hollow cone | On axis, $\frac{h}{3}$ from base |

Where $h$ = height of cone.

---

### ✅ Plumb Line Method — Key Points

- Principle: When suspended, CG lies directly below the point of suspension.
- Need minimum **2 lines** (3 recommended to verify).
- CG is at the **intersection of the plumb lines**.
- Works for any irregular lamina.
- If CG is outside the lamina (like a ring), the intersection point will be in empty space.

---

### ✅ Stability

| Type | Condition | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Stable** | CG line falls within the base | Wide-based objects, low CG |
| **Unstable** | CG line falls outside the base → topples | Tall narrow objects with high CG |
| **Neutral** | CG height doesn't change when displaced | A ball on a flat surface |

> **ICSE Fact:** A double-decker bus does not topple in normal conditions because the chassis (base) is designed very wide and the heavy engine is kept low — maintaining a low CG with the vertical line always within the base.

## ⚠️ Newton Cat's Exam Traps

> 🐱 *Newton the Cat tried to balance a ring on its CG. He reached into the hole and found nothing. He panicked.*

**Trap 1 — "CG must be inside the material of the object"**
> ❌ **WRONG.** CG can be in empty space — inside the hole of a ring, between the arms of a boomerang, or outside some L-shaped brackets. For an L-shape, its exact position depends on the dimensions and mass distribution.

**Trap 2 — "CG of a cone is at its midpoint (h/2)"**
> ❌ **WRONG.** Solid cone: $h/4$ from base. Hollow cone: $h/3$ from base. The mass distribution is not uniform along the height — more material is near the base.

**Trap 3 — "Any two plumb lines are enough to confirm the CG"**
> ⚠️ **PARTIALLY WRONG.** Technically 2 lines determine a unique point. But in practice, a third line is needed to **verify** (experimental error). ICSE practicals require 3 holes.

**Trap 4 — "A heavier object is always more stable"**
> ❌ **WRONG.** Stability depends on the CG height and the base width, not the mass. A heavy tall vase is less stable than a light flat plate.

**Trap 5 — "CG is the same as the geometric centre always"**
> ❌ Only for **uniform** objects with regular shapes. For non-uniform objects (denser on one side, or hollow shapes), the CG shifts away from the geometric centre toward the heavier region.
