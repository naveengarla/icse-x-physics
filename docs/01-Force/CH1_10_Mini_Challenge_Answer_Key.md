## Section 1.1 - Translational and Rotational Motion

### Conceptual

The spinning top has combined motion. It is rotating about its own axis and also translating because its centre moves across the floor.

### Numerical

(a) The rod undergoes rotational motion about the pivot.

(b) The midpoint is half as far from the pivot as the free end, so it covers half the arc length in the same time.

Free-end speed = 10 cm/s  
Midpoint speed = 5 cm/s

### Application

(a) The door undergoes rotational motion about the hinge.

(b) Pushing at the far edge gives a larger perpendicular distance from the hinge, so the moment of force is larger.

(c) Turning effect depends on both force and perpendicular distance from the pivot.

## Section 1.2 - Moment of Force

### Conceptual

Mechanic 1:

$$M = 20 \times 0.25 = 5 \text{ N m}$$

Mechanic 2:

$$M = 10 \times 0.60 = 6 \text{ N m}$$

Mechanic 2 is more effective because the larger distance more than compensates for the smaller force.

### Numerical

Pivot = 50 cm mark.

Distance of 5 N from pivot:

$$50 - 20 = 30 \text{ cm} = 0.30 \text{ m}$$

Distance of $F$ from pivot:

$$80 - 50 = 30 \text{ cm} = 0.30 \text{ m}$$

For equilibrium:

$$5 \times 0.30 = F \times 0.30$$

$$F = 5 \text{ N}$$

### Application

(a)

$$d = \frac{\tau}{F} = \frac{1.5}{3} = 0.5 \text{ m}$$

(b)

$$F = \frac{\tau}{d} = \frac{1.5}{0.40} = 3.75 \text{ N}$$

(c)

$$\tau = 3 \times 0.60 = 1.8 \text{ N m}$$

Yes, because 1.8 N m is greater than the required 1.5 N m.

## Section 1.4 - Equilibrium

### Conceptual

(a) Static equilibrium.

(b) Forces are weight downward and contact/tension forces from the rope upward. Their resultant must be zero.

(c) Net moment must also be zero; otherwise she would rotate and fall.

(d) If she leans, the vertical line through her CG may shift outside the support region, producing a toppling moment.

### Numerical

Pivot = 40 cm.

200 gf at 10 cm:

$$d = 40 - 10 = 30 \text{ cm}$$

Moment:

$$200 \times 30 = 6000 \text{ gf cm}$$

The ruler's weight is 100 gf acting at 50 cm:

$$100 \times 10 = 1000 \text{ gf cm}$$

Let force at 70 cm be $F$:

$$d = 70 - 40 = 30 \text{ cm}$$

For rotational equilibrium:

$$6000 = 1000 + 30F$$

$$F = 166.7 \text{ gf}$$

The support reaction balances the total downward force for translational equilibrium.

### Application

(a) Resistive forces = 80 N.

(b) Dynamic equilibrium.

(c) No. If pedalling stops, forward driving force decreases, resistive force remains backward, and the cyclist slows down.

(d) Once stopped on level ground with balanced forces, the cyclist is in static equilibrium.

## Section 1.6 - Centre of Gravity

### Conceptual

(a) For uniform upright solid and hollow cylinders of the same height, the CG of each is at the midpoint of the height.

(b) Hollowness alone does not change CG height if the cylinder is symmetric and uniform along its height.

(c) If water is added at the bottom, the combined CG shifts downward.

### Numerical

This one needs a clear coordinate origin and the exact position of the cut-out rectangle. If the 2 cm x 4 cm rectangle is removed from the bottom-right corner of a 6 cm x 6 cm square, take the square's lower-left corner as origin:

Full square:
- Area = 36 cm2
- CG = (3, 3)

Removed rectangle:
- Area = 8 cm2
- CG = (5, 2)

Remaining L-shape:

$$A = 36 - 8 = 28$$

$$\bar{x} = \frac{36(3) - 8(5)}{28} = \frac{68}{28} = 2.43 \text{ cm}$$

$$\bar{y} = \frac{36(3) - 8(2)}{28} = \frac{92}{28} = 3.29 \text{ cm}$$

CG = (2.43 cm, 3.29 cm) from the lower-left corner.

Whether this lies inside the material depends on the exact cut-out location and L-shape geometry.

### Application

Take the 8000 kg as the bus body mass, with its CG at 1.2 m.

Combined CG height:

$$h_{CG} = \frac{8000(1.2) + 2000(1.0) + 1500(3.5)}{8000 + 2000 + 1500}$$

$$h_{CG} = \frac{9600 + 2000 + 5250}{11500} = 1.47 \text{ m approximately}$$

For toppling:

Half track width = 1.2 m.

$$\tan \theta = \frac{1.2}{1.47} = 0.816$$

$$\theta \approx 39^\circ$$

Sand bags near the floor lower the CG, increasing stability.

## Section 1.7 - Uniform Circular Motion

### Conceptual

(a) Velocity is tangential to the circular path.

(b) Acceleration is toward the centre.

(c) When the string breaks, the stone moves along the tangent at the point of release.

(d) No. It is accelerating toward the centre, so net force is not zero.

### Numerical

(a)

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{6^2}{2} = 18 \text{ m/s}^2$$

(b)

$$F_c = ma_c = 0.5 \times 18 = 9 \text{ N}$$

(c) If speed doubles, force becomes four times:

$$F_c = 36 \text{ N}$$

(d) If radius halves, force doubles:

$$F_c = 18 \text{ N}$$

### Application

(a) No. The cyclist is not in equilibrium because direction of velocity changes continuously.

(b)

$$a_c = \frac{10^2}{50} = 2 \text{ m/s}^2$$

(c) Friction between tyres and road provides the centripetal force.

(d) If friction is too low, the required centripetal force is unavailable and the cyclist skids outward from the circular path.

## Section 1.8 - Centripetal and Centrifugal Force

### Conceptual

(a) From a traffic camera, the passenger tends to continue in a straight line due to inertia while the car turns right.

(b) The passenger presses against the left door.

(c) The force from the door on the passenger is centripetal, directed toward the centre of the turn.

(d) From inside the car, the passenger feels pushed left/outward. This is apparent centrifugal force, not a real force in the inertial frame.

### Numerical

(a)

$$a_c = \frac{4^2}{0.8} = 20 \text{ m/s}^2$$

(b)

$$F_c = ma_c = 0.2 \times 20 = 4 \text{ N}$$

(c) If speed doubles, force becomes four times:

$$F_c = 16 \text{ N}$$

(d)

$$F_c = \frac{0.2 \times 4^2}{1.6} = 2 \text{ N}$$

### Application

(a)

$$54 \text{ km/h} = 15 \text{ m/s}$$

(b)

$$F_c = \frac{1200 \times 15^2}{80} = 3375 \text{ N}$$

(c) Friction between tyres and road provides the centripetal force.

(d) Yes. Available friction = 4000 N, which is greater than 3375 N.

(e)

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{F_c r}{m}}$$

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{4000 \times 80}{1200}} = 16.33 \text{ m/s}$$

In km/h:

$$16.33 \times 3.6 = 58.8 \text{ km/h}$$

Maximum safe speed = about 58.8 km/h.

### B. Two-Mark Reasoning Questions

1. Work = force x displacement in the direction of force, while moment = force x perpendicular distance from a pivot. They have the same dimensions but different meanings, so moment is written as N m, not joule.

2. Its CG lies vertically below the point of suspension. In that position, the line of action of weight passes through the suspension point, so no turning moment acts.

3. If a numerical value is given, the answer must be numerical. For example, for a hollow cone of height 9 cm, writing only $h/3$ is incomplete; the value $3$ cm from the base is expected.

4. The stone moves along the tangent at the point where the string breaks. This happens because the centripetal force is removed and the stone continues in its instantaneous direction of motion.

5. Work done is zero. Centripetal force is directed toward the centre while displacement at that instant is along the tangent; the angle between them is 90 degrees.

6. A broad base and low heavy parts make the vertical line through the CG remain inside the base for a larger tilt. This increases stability and reduces the chance of toppling.

7. The reaction at the knife edge acts through the pivot. Its perpendicular distance from the pivot is zero, so its moment about the knife edge is zero.

8. A couple consists of equal and opposite forces, so resultant force is zero and there is no translation. Since their lines of action are different, their moments add and produce rotation.

### C. Three-Mark Application Questions

1. For a hollow cone, CG is at $h/3$ from the broad base.

$$\frac{9}{3} = 3 \text{ cm}$$

Yes, the position changes when completely filled with honey because the mass distribution changes. The filled cone behaves like a solid cone if uniformly filled, so its CG shifts to $h/4$ from the base:

$$\frac{9}{4} = 2.25 \text{ cm}$$

2. For a solid cone, CG is at $h/4$ from the base.

$$\frac{12}{4} = 3 \text{ cm}$$

For a hollow cone of the same height:

$$\frac{12}{3} = 4 \text{ cm}$$

So the hollow cone's CG is farther from the base than the solid cone's CG.

3. Draw one plumb line when suspended from A and another when suspended from B. The intersection point of the two plumb lines gives the CG. Each plumb line passes through the CG because a freely suspended body rests with its CG vertically below the point of suspension.

4.

$$M = Fd$$

$$d = \frac{M}{F} = \frac{10}{50} = 0.2 \text{ m}$$

5.

$$d = 25 \text{ cm} = 0.25 \text{ m}$$

### D. Metre-Rule Numericals

#### 1. Metre rule, pivot at 30 cm

Data:
- Rule weight = 50 gf acting at 50 cm
- 40 gf at 5 cm
- Unknown $W$ at 80 cm
- Pivot = 30 cm

Moments:
- 40 gf: distance $30 - 5 = 25$ cm, ACW
- Rule: distance $50 - 30 = 20$ cm, CW
- Unknown: distance $80 - 30 = 50$ cm, CW

$$40 \times 25 = 50 \times 20 + W \times 50$$

$$1000 = 1000 + 50W$$

$$W = 0$$

No extra weight is needed at 80 cm; the 40 gf load is already balanced by the rule's own weight.

#### 2. Metre rule, pivot at 40 cm

Data:
- Rule mass = 100 g acting at 50 cm
- 200 g at 10 cm
- Unknown $m$ at 70 cm
- Pivot = 40 cm

Moments:
- 200 g: distance 30 cm, ACW
- Rule: distance 10 cm, CW
- Unknown: distance 30 cm, CW

$$200 \times 30 = 100 \times 10 + m \times 30$$

$$6000 = 1000 + 30m$$

$$m = 166.7 \text{ g}$$

#### 3. Metre rule balancing at 29 cm

Uniform metre rule has CG at 50 cm.

Data:
- Pivot = 29 cm
- 20 gf at 0 cm
- Rule weight = $W$ at 50 cm

Moments:
- 20 gf at 0 cm: distance 29 cm, ACW
- Rule weight at 50 cm: distance 21 cm, CW

For equilibrium:

$$20 \times 29 = W \times 21$$

$$W = \frac{580}{21} = 27.6 \text{ gf approximately}$$

Weight of the metre rule = 27.6 gf.

#### 4. Centre pivot with three loads

Pivot = 50 cm.

Moments:
- 60 gf at 20 cm: distance 30 cm, ACW
- 40 gf at 90 cm: distance 40 cm, CW
- 80 gf must be placed on the left or right depending on shortage.

Existing ACW moment:

$$60 \times 30 = 1800 \text{ gf cm}$$

Existing CW moment:

$$40 \times 40 = 1600 \text{ gf cm}$$

Need extra CW moment:

$$1800 - 1600 = 200 \text{ gf cm}$$

For 80 gf:

$$80d = 200$$

$$d = 2.5 \text{ cm}$$

Place it 2.5 cm to the right of the pivot:

$$50 + 2.5 = 52.5 \text{ cm mark}$$

#### 5. Pivot at 60 cm

Data:
- Rule weight = 80 gf at 50 cm
- 100 gf at 20 cm
- Unknown $W$ at 90 cm
- Pivot = 60 cm

Moments:
- Rule: distance 10 cm, ACW
- 100 gf: distance 40 cm, ACW
- Unknown: distance 30 cm, CW

$$80 \times 10 + 100 \times 40 = W \times 30$$

$$800 + 4000 = 30W$$

$$W = 160 \text{ gf}$$

### F. Diagram-Based Prompts - Marking Points

1. Suspended brass ball:
- Downward arrow: weight of ball
- Upward arrow: tension in string
- Arrows collinear and opposite
- Labels clear

2. Metre rule:
- Knife edge/pivot marked
- All weights shown downward
- Rule's own weight shown at 50 cm if uniform
- Distances measured from pivot, not from zero mark
- CW and ACW directions labelled

3. Circular motion:
- Radius line from centre to body
- Velocity tangent to circle
- Centripetal force toward centre
- If string breaks, path shown tangent

4. Plumb-line method:
- Irregular lamina suspended from at least two holes
- Plumb lines drawn vertically from suspension point
- Intersection marked CG
- Labels: suspension point, plumb line, CG
