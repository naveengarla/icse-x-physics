## What is this chapter about?

Have you ever wondered why a door handle is always placed far from the hinges? Or why a long spanner makes it so much easier to loosen a tight nut? Or why the moon keeps circling the Earth without flying off into space?

All of these involve **force** — not just the pushing-and-pulling kind, but forces that *spin* things, *balance* things, and keep objects moving in circles. This chapter takes you from spinning wheels and turning doors all the way to planets orbiting the sun. By the end, you will see physics at work in almost everything around you!

The chapter is divided into three big ideas:

- **(A) Turning Forces** — how forces spin things, and how balance works
- **(B) Centre of Gravity** — why every object has one special "balance point"
- **(C) Uniform Circular Motion** — why moving in a circle always means accelerating

## Section Notes — Definitions, Traps and Challenges

### § 1.1 — Translational and Rotational Motions

**Definition — Translational Motion**
> Motion in which every particle of the body undergoes the **same displacement** — same magnitude and direction — in a given interval of time. All particles share identical velocity at every instant.

**Definition — Rotational Motion**
> Motion in which every particle moves along a **circular arc**, all arcs having their centres on a common axis (pivot). Particles at different distances from the pivot trace arcs of different radii and have different speeds.

| Feature | Translational | Rotational |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| All particles same velocity? | Yes | No |
| Path traced | Parallel (same) | Circles of different radii |
| Requires fixed pivot? | No | Not always — but ICSE examples use one |
| Example | Book sliding on desk | Door opening on hinge |

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- A spinning cricket ball travelling across a pitch is in **combined motion** (both translational and rotational) — not purely rotational.
- The pivot stays fixed because it is *constrained* (held by hinge/wall) — weight has nothing to do with it.
- Translation does not mean "straight line only" — curvilinear paths count too.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A spinning top placed on a smooth floor spins about its tip AND slowly drifts sideways. Is this translational, rotational, or combined? Justify.

### § 1.2 — Moment of Force (Torque)

**Definition**
> The **moment of force** (torque) about a point is the product of the magnitude of the force and the **perpendicular distance** of the line of action of the force from that point.

$$\tau = F \times d$$

- SI unit: **N m** (not Joule — they are dimensionally equal but physically different)
- CGS unit: dyne·cm &ensp; (1 N m = 10⁷ dyne·cm)

**Sign Convention**
| Rotation | Sign |
|:---|:---:|
| Anticlockwise (ACW) | Positive (+) |
| Clockwise (CW) | Negative (−) |

**Factors affecting moment of force**
1. Magnitude of force F — larger F → larger moment
2. Perpendicular distance d — larger d → larger moment

**ICSE Quick-Reference**

| Situation | What it shows |
|:---|:---|
| Door handle far from hinge | Large d → small F needed |
| Long spanner / wrench | Large d → more torque on bolt |
| Bicycle pedal on large crank | Crank length = perpendicular distance |
| Force applied at pivot | d = 0 → moment = zero, no rotation possible |

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- **Torque ≠ Work.** Both have unit N m but they are completely different. Work is scalar, torque is a vector. Never write "Joule" for moment of force.
- **d is NOT the distance from hand to pivot.** It is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the *line of action* of the force.
- A force of any magnitude applied **directly at the pivot** produces **zero** moment.
- Specify CW or ACW in answers — direction matters for full marks.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> Mechanic A applies 20 N at the end of a 25 cm spanner. Mechanic B applies 10 N with a 60 cm spanner.
> (a) Calculate the torque each produces. (b) Who is more effective? (c) What does this tell you about force vs distance?

### § 1.3 — Couple

**Definition**
> A **couple** consists of two **equal**, **parallel** forces acting in **opposite directions** along **different lines of action**.

$$\tau_{\text{couple}} = F \times d$$

where d = perpendicular distance between the two forces (the **couple arm**).

**Key Properties**

| Property | Value |
|:---|:---|
| Net force | Zero (no translational motion) |
| Net moment | Non-zero: F × d (pure rotation) |
| Moment about any reference point | **Same** — reference-independent |
| Can be balanced by | Only another couple of equal and opposite moment |

**Common examples**

| Application | Couple arm |
|:---|:---|
| Water tap | Diameter of knob |
| Steering wheel | Diameter of wheel |
| Screwdriver | Width of handle |
| Key in lock | Width of key grip |

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- Equal and opposite forces cancel to zero *only if along the same line.* Different lines → they add rotation.
- Moment of couple = F × d, NOT 2F × d. The couple arm d is already the *full* separation.
- A couple **cannot be balanced by a single force** — a single force always has a translational component.
- The moment of a couple is **the same about any point** in its plane.
- A couple is **not** an action-reaction pair. An action-reaction pair acts on *different* bodies; a couple acts on the *same* body.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> Two forces of 8 N each act as a couple on a wheel of diameter 40 cm.
> (a) What is the couple arm? (b) Calculate the moment. (c) What motion results?

### § 1.4 — Equilibrium of Bodies

**Definition**
> A body is said to be in **equilibrium** if it does not change its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line.

**Kinds of Equilibrium**

| Type | Condition | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Static** | Body at rest, net F = 0, net τ = 0 | Book on table, balanced metre rule |
| **Dynamic** | Constant velocity (≠0), net F = 0, net τ = 0 | Raindrop at terminal velocity, car at constant speed |

**Two Conditions for Equilibrium**

1. **First condition (translational):** $\sum \vec{F} = 0$ — vector sum of all forces = zero
2. **Second condition (rotational):** $\sum \tau = 0$ — algebraic sum of all moments = zero

| Feature | Static | Dynamic |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| Body at rest? | Yes | No |
| Constant velocity? | Yes (= 0) | Yes (≠ 0) |
| Net force? | Zero | Zero |
| Net moment? | Zero | Zero |
| Acceleration? | None | None |

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- Equilibrium does **not** mean the body is stationary — dynamic equilibrium means constant velocity.
- A ball at the top of its arc has zero velocity but gravity still acts → **not** equilibrium.
- Net force = 0 satisfies only the **first** condition. You must also verify net moment = 0.
- A satellite in circular orbit has constantly changing direction → net force ≠ 0 → **not** equilibrium.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A tightrope walker stands perfectly still on the rope.
> (a) What type of equilibrium? (b) List all forces and explain why net force = 0. (c) Why must net moment also = 0?

### § 1.5 — Principle of Moments

**Statement**
> If a body is in rotational equilibrium, the sum of the anticlockwise moments about any point equals the sum of the clockwise moments about that same point.

$$\sum \tau_{\text{ACW}} = \sum \tau_{\text{CW}}$$

**Verification experiment** — metre rule on a pivot, known weights on both sides, adjust until horizontal, verify that sum of CW moments = sum of ACW moments.

**Units in ICSE**

| Quantity | SI unit | ICSE practical unit |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| Force (weight) | N | gf or kgf |
| Distance | m | cm |
| Moment | N m | gf·cm or kgf·m |

> In theory questions, give SI (N m). In practicals, gf·cm is acceptable.

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- Unless told "massless," always include the ruler's own weight acting at its midpoint (50 cm for uniform ruler).
- Distance for moments = distance **from the pivot**, not the marked position on the ruler.
- The side a force is on does not automatically determine CW or ACW — always draw and check visually.
- Never mix gf·cm and N·m in the same calculation. Pick one system and convert everything.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A uniform metre rule (mass 80 g) is pivoted at 20 cm. Weights placed: 60 g at 5 cm, 100 g at 50 cm, unknown mass m at 0 cm.
> (a) Find the moment of each force about the pivot. (b) Identify CW/ACW for each. (c) Find m for equilibrium.

### § 1.6 — Centre of Gravity

**Definition**
> The **centre of gravity (CG)** of a body is the point through which the resultant of the gravitational forces on all particles acts, irrespective of the orientation of the body.

*Equivalently: the single point where the entire weight of the body is assumed to act.*

**CG of Regular Uniform Objects**

| Object | Position of CG |
|:---|:---|
| Uniform straight rod | Midpoint of the rod |
| Rectangular / square lamina | Intersection of diagonals |
| Circular disc | Geometric centre |
| Circular ring | Centre of the ring *(in empty space!)* |
| Triangular lamina | Intersection of medians (centroid) |
| Solid sphere | Geometric centre |
| Solid cylinder | Midpoint of its axis |
| Solid cone | h/4 from base on axis |
| Hollow cone | h/3 from base on axis |

**Plumb Line Method (for irregular lamina)**
- Principle: when suspended, the CG lies directly **below** the point of suspension
- Make **3 holes** near the edge, hang from each, draw vertical line through each hole
- CG = intersection of all three lines
- *(2 lines are theoretically enough, but a 3rd verifies against experimental error)*

**Stability**

| Type | Condition | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Stable** | CG line falls within base | Wide-based objects, low CG |
| **Unstable** | CG line falls outside base → topples | Tall narrow objects |
| **Neutral** | CG height unchanged when displaced | Ball on flat surface |

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- CG can be **outside the material** of a body — inside the hole of a ring, gap of a boomerang, or outside an L-bracket.
- Solid cone CG = **h/4** from base. Hollow cone = **h/3** from base. NOT h/2.
- ICSE practicals require **3 holes**, not 2.
- A heavier object is **not automatically** more stable — stability depends on CG height and base width, not mass.
- CG = geometric centre only for **uniform** regular objects. Non-uniform objects shift CG toward the heavier region.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A solid cylinder and a hollow cylinder: same mass, same height, same outer radius, both uniform.
> (a) Where is the CG of each? (b) Does hollowness change the CG height in this symmetric case? (c) If water fills the hollow cylinder's bottom, how does the CG change?

### § 1.7 — Uniform Circular Motion

**Definition**
> **Uniform circular motion** is the motion of a body moving along a circular path with **constant speed**.

- Speed is constant; *velocity* is NOT constant (direction changes continuously)
- The body is continuously accelerating even though its speed is unchanged
- Velocity direction at any instant = along the **tangent** to the circle at that point

**UCM vs Uniform Linear Motion**

| Feature | Uniform Linear | Uniform Circular |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| Speed | Constant | Constant |
| Velocity | Constant (no change) | Variable (direction changes) |
| Acceleration | **Zero** | **Non-zero** (centripetal) |
| Net force | **Zero** | **Non-zero** (centripetal force) |
| Path | Straight line | Circle |
| Newton's Law | 1st Law | 2nd Law |

**Centripetal Acceleration**

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$

Direction: always toward the **centre** of the circle.

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- "Constant speed" does **not** mean zero acceleration. Acceleration = rate of change of *velocity*. Direction change = acceleration.
- When the string breaks, the object flies along the **tangent** — not radially outward.
- Centripetal acceleration points **inward** (toward centre), never outward.
- UCM is fundamentally different from uniform linear motion — do not confuse them.
- A satellite is weightless because it is in **free fall**, not because gravity is absent.

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A ball moves in a circle of radius 2 m at constant speed 6 m/s.
> (a) Calculate centripetal acceleration. (b) If mass = 0.5 kg, find centripetal force. (c) Speed doubles — new centripetal force? (d) Speed same but radius halves — new centripetal force?

### § 1.8 — Centripetal and Centrifugal Force

**Centripetal Force**
> The force required to keep a body moving in a circular path, directed **toward the centre** of the circle.

$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Key properties:
- Always directed **toward the centre**; always **perpendicular** to velocity
- It is **not a new force** — it is whichever existing force (tension / gravity / friction) provides the inward pull
- It changes the **direction** of velocity, not the speed

**What provides centripetal force?**

| Situation | Source of centripetal force |
|:---|:---|
| Moon orbiting Earth | Gravitational pull by Earth |
| Planet orbiting Sun | Gravitational pull by Sun |
| Electron around nucleus | Electrostatic attraction by nucleus |
| Stone on a string | Tension in the string |
| Car rounding a bend | Friction from road on tyres |

**Centrifugal Force**
> An **apparent (fictitious/pseudo) force** experienced in a rotating (non-inertial) reference frame, directed **away from the centre**.

- NOT a real force — no physical agent exerts it
- A consequence of inertia being misinterpreted in a rotating frame
- Disappears when the observer steps into an inertial (non-rotating) frame
- Equal in magnitude to centripetal force, but apparently outward

**Comparison Table**

| Feature | Centripetal | Centrifugal |
|:---|:---:|:---:|
| Real or fictitious? | **Real** | **Fictitious** |
| Direction | Toward centre | Away from centre |
| Frame of reference | Inertial (outside observer) | Non-inertial (rotating observer) |
| Newton's Law | 2nd Law | Pseudo-force |
| Action-reaction pair? | **No** | **No** |

**Important ICSE statements**
1. Centripetal force does **no work** on the body (force is perpendicular to displacement).
2. When centripetal force is removed, body moves along the **tangent**, not outward.
3. Centripetal and centrifugal are **NOT** an action-reaction pair.

**🐱 Exam Traps**
- Centrifugal force is **not real** — always call it *fictitious / apparent / pseudo* in ICSE answers.
- Centripetal + centrifugal are **not** action-reaction pairs. Both appear to act on the same body; centrifugal is not a real interaction force.
- String breaks → stone flies along **tangent**, not radially outward.
- Never draw a separate "centripetal force" arrow in force diagrams — identify which real force (tension/gravity/friction) provides it.
- Doubling speed **quadruples** centripetal force (because v is squared in F = mv²/r).

**🧩 Mini Challenge**
> A passenger sits on the left side of a car. The car makes a sharp right turn.
> (a) Which direction does the passenger tend to move — from a traffic camera above? (b) Which door does the shoulder press against? (c) Is the force on the passenger from the door centripetal or centrifugal? (d) From the passenger's perspective, what force do they feel, and is it real?
