## Topics Covered

### 12.1 The Nucleus
- Atom: electrons orbit the nucleus; nucleus contains protons and neutrons
- **Proton number (Z)**: number of protons = atomic number
- **Nucleon number (A)**: total protons + neutrons = mass number
- **Neutrons**: `N = A − Z`
- **Isotopes**: atoms of same element with same Z but different A (e.g., ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C)

### 12.2 Radioactivity
- **Radioactivity**: spontaneous emission of radiation from the nucleus of an unstable atom
- Discovered by **Henri Becquerel** (1896); studied extensively by **Marie and Pierre Curie**
- Radioactivity is a **nuclear** property — unaffected by temperature, pressure, chemical state
- **Radioactive decay**: unstable nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation → becomes more stable

### 12.3 Types of Radioactive Radiation

| Property | Alpha (α) | Beta (β) | Gamma (γ) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nature | Helium nucleus (²⁴He) | Fast electron (⁰₋₁e) | EM radiation (photon) |
| Charge | +2e | −1e | 0 |
| Mass | 4 u | ~1/2000 u | 0 |
| Speed | ~5% of c | up to 90% of c | c (3×10⁸ m/s) |
| Penetrating power | Least (stopped by paper / few cm air) | Medium (stopped by few mm Al) | Highest (reduced by thick Pb or concrete) |
| Ionising power | Highest | Medium | Least |
| Effect of electric/magnetic field | Deflected | Deflected (opposite to α) | Not deflected |

### 12.4 Nuclear Equations
- Mass number and atomic number are both conserved
- **Alpha decay**: `²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He`
- **Beta decay**: `¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e`
- **Gamma emission**: accompanies alpha or beta decay (no change in Z or A)

### 12.5 Half-Life (T½)
- **Half-life**: time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay
- After each half-life, activity and number of undecayed nuclei halve
- After n half-lives: `N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ`
- Half-life is **constant** for a given isotope — unaffected by external conditions

| Isotope | Half-life | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon-14 | 5,730 years | Carbon dating of fossils/artefacts |
| Uranium-238 | 4.5 × 10⁹ years | Dating rocks / Earth's age |
| Iodine-131 | 8 days | Thyroid diagnosis and treatment |
| Cobalt-60 | 5.27 years | Radiotherapy for cancer |
| Radon-222 | 3.8 days | Background radiation |

### 12.6 Background Radiation
- Low-level radiation present everywhere; mostly natural
- Sources: cosmic rays, radon gas from ground, radioactive rocks, food, medical X-rays
- Must be subtracted when measuring activity of a sample

### 12.7 Uses of Radioactivity

#### Medical Uses
- **Radiotherapy**: γ-rays from Co-60 to kill cancer cells
- **Radioactive tracers**: I-131 for thyroid function; Tc-99m for organ imaging
- **Sterilisation**: gamma rays sterilise surgical instruments without heating

#### Industrial Uses
- **Thickness gauging**: β particles measure thickness of paper/metal sheets
- **Leak detection**: radioactive tracer injected; leak detected by radiation outside pipe
- **Food irradiation**: gamma rays kill bacteria → extend shelf life
- **Smoke detectors**: Am-241 (alpha emitter) ionises air; smoke disrupts ionisation → alarm

#### Scientific Uses
- **Carbon dating**: C-14 used to find age of organic materials up to ~50,000 years
- **Uranium-lead dating**: for geological samples (millions to billions of years)

### 12.8 Safety Precautions
- Use tongs / remote-controlled handling — never touch with bare hands
- Store in **lead-lined containers**
- Wear lead aprons, lead-glass goggles, dosimeters
- Minimise exposure time; maximise distance from source
- No eating, drinking or smoking near radioactive materials
- Dispose of radioactive waste safely (deep burial in concrete/lead containers)

### 12.9 Nuclear Energy

#### Nuclear Fission
- Splitting of a heavy nucleus (e.g., U-235) into two lighter nuclei + energy + neutrons
- Example: `²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ⁹²₃₆Kr + ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + 3¹₀n + energy`
- Energy released = mass defect × c² (`E = mc²`)
- **Chain reaction**: neutrons produced trigger further fissions
  - **Controlled**: nuclear reactor (slow neutrons; control rods absorb excess neutrons)
  - **Uncontrolled**: atomic bomb
- **Nuclear Reactor components**:
  - **Fuel**: enriched U-235 or Pu-239 (fuel rods)
  - **Moderator**: slows neutrons (heavy water, graphite)
  - **Control rods**: absorb neutrons to control reaction rate (boron or cadmium)
  - **Coolant**: removes heat (water, CO₂)
  - **Shielding**: thick concrete/lead to absorb radiation

#### Nuclear Fusion
- Combining two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus + enormous energy
- Example: `²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + energy`
- Requires temperature ~10⁷ K (thermonuclear reaction)
- **Powers the sun and stars**
- More energy than fission; fuel (hydrogen from sea water) is abundant; less radioactive waste
- **Fusion bomb** (hydrogen bomb): uncontrolled fusion
- Controlled fusion for power generation: under research (ITER project)

### 12.10 X-Rays
- High-energy EM radiation; wavelength 0.01–10 nm
- **Production**: high-speed electrons suddenly decelerated when hitting a metal target (anode)
- **Properties**: travel at c; ionise matter; affect photographic film; penetrate matter
- **Uses**:
  - Medical: skeletal imaging, chest X-rays, CT scans, cancer treatment (radiotherapy)
  - Industrial: detecting internal cracks and weld quality
  - Security: airport baggage scanners
- **Hazards**: ionise cells → can cause cancer; precautions: lead aprons, limited exposure

## Key Formulae

| Quantity | Formula |
|---|---|
| Remaining nuclei after n half-lives | `N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ` |
| Mass–energy equivalence | `E = mc²` |
| Nucleon/mass number conservation | `A₁ = A₂ + A₃ + …` |
| Atomic number conservation | `Z₁ = Z₂ + Z₃ + …` |

---

## Important Terms

- **Radioactivity**: Spontaneous emission of radiation from an unstable nucleus
- **Half-life**: Time for half of radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay
- **Alpha particle**: Helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons); least penetrating
- **Nuclear Fission**: Splitting of heavy nucleus releasing energy
- **Nuclear Fusion**: Joining of light nuclei releasing enormous energy
- **Chain Reaction**: Self-sustaining sequence of fission reactions
