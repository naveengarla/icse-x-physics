## Topics Covered

### 10.1 Magnetic Effect of Electric Current
- **Oersted's Experiment (1820)**: a current-carrying conductor deflects a nearby compass needle → current produces a magnetic field
- **Magnetic field lines** around a straight conductor: concentric circles; direction given by the **Right-Hand Thumb Rule**
  - Thumb points in direction of current → fingers curl in direction of field lines

### 10.2 Magnetic Field of a Solenoid
- A solenoid (coil of wire) carrying current behaves like a **bar magnet**
- Magnetic field inside is uniform and strong; outside, field is like that of a bar magnet
- End with field lines emerging = North pole; end with field lines entering = South pole
- Right-hand grip rule: curl fingers in direction of current → thumb points to North pole

### 10.3 Electromagnet
- An iron core placed inside a solenoid becomes magnetised when current flows → electromagnet
- **Factors affecting strength**:
  - Number of turns in coil (more turns → stronger)
  - Current magnitude (higher current → stronger)
  - Nature of core (soft iron → stronger, demagnetises quickly; steel → weaker, permanent)
- **Uses**: electric bells, cranes (scrap yards), relays, MRI machines, circuit breakers

### 10.4 Electric Bell
- **Working**: current → electromagnet activates → attracts iron armature → hammer strikes gong → circuit breaks → armature springs back → circuit completes → repeats
- Key parts: electromagnet, armature, spring, contact screw, hammer, gong, battery

### 10.5 Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field
- A conductor carrying current in a magnetic field experiences a force (motor effect)
- Formula: `F = BIL sin θ`
  - B = magnetic field strength (Tesla), I = current (A), L = length of conductor (m)
  - Maximum force when θ = 90° (conductor ⊥ field): `F = BIL`
  - Zero force when θ = 0° (conductor ∥ field)
- **Fleming's Left-Hand Rule** (FBI rule):
  - **F**irst finger → magnetic **F**ield (B)
  - Se**C**ond finger → **C**urrent (I)  *(or: seCond finger → Current)*
  - Thu**M**b → **M**otion (Force on conductor)

### 10.6 Electric Motor (DC Motor)
- **Principle**: current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences torque → rotation
- **Key components**: rectangular coil (armature), permanent magnet, commutator (split ring), brushes
- **Working**: current through coil → force on each side (Fleming's LHR) → torque rotates coil → commutator reverses current every half turn → continuous rotation in same direction
- **Uses**: electric fans, mixers, washing machines, electric trains, pumps, drills

### 10.7 Electromagnetic Induction
- **Faraday's discovery**: a changing magnetic field through a coil induces an EMF in it
- **Faraday's Laws**:
  1. An EMF is induced whenever the magnetic flux through a circuit changes
  2. Magnitude of induced EMF ∝ rate of change of magnetic flux
- **Lenz's Law**: induced current opposes the change causing it (conservation of energy)
- **Fleming's Right-Hand Rule** (for generator):
  - **F**irst finger → magnetic **F**ield
  - Thu**M**b → **M**otion of conductor
  - Se**C**ond finger → induced **C**urrent

### 10.8 AC Generator
- **Principle**: electromagnetic induction (coil rotating in magnetic field)
- **Components**: rectangular coil, strong permanent magnet, slip rings (two), brushes
- **Output**: alternating current (sinusoidal); direction reverses every half rotation
- India: AC frequency = 50 Hz; voltage = 230 V

### 10.9 DC Generator
- Same as AC generator but uses a **commutator** (split ring) instead of slip rings
- Output: pulsating DC (same direction always)

| Feature | AC Generator | DC Generator |
|---|---|---|
| Collection device | Slip rings | Commutator |
| Output | AC | Pulsating DC |
| Use | Power stations | Vehicles, small devices |

### 10.10 Transformer
- **Principle**: mutual electromagnetic induction between two coils
- **Components**: primary coil, secondary coil, soft iron core (laminated)
- **Transformer equation**: `Vs/Vp = Ns/Np = Ip/Is`
- **Step-up transformer**: Ns > Np → Vs > Vp (increases voltage, decreases current)
- **Step-down transformer**: Ns < Np → Vs < Vp (decreases voltage, increases current)
- **Efficiency**: `η = (Vs × Is)/(Vp × Ip) × 100%` (ideal = 100%)
- **Why AC for transmission**: voltage can be stepped up (reduces `I²R` loss over long distances)
- **Energy losses in transformers**: eddy currents (minimised by lamination), hysteresis, resistance of coils
